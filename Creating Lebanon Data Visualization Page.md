## Legal framework for the digital age

### Data protection and electronic transactions

Lebanon adopted Law 81/2018 on Electronic Transactions and Personal Data, which legally recognises electronic signatures and documents and establishes a framework for personal-data processing. Article 87 and related provisions require that personal data be collected for legitimate, specific purposes and be adequate, relevant, and not excessive, and they grant individuals rights of information, access, and correction.[26][27][28]

However, civil-society and expert analyses argue that the law is outdated and incomplete. SMEX describes Law 81/2018’s data-protection chapter as "ugly", concentrating power in the executive branch, omitting modern safeguards inspired by frameworks like the GDPR, and lagging far behind global norms. Freedom on the Net 2024 similarly notes that Lebanon still lacks comprehensive data-protection legislation and effective enforcement, and that courts, rather than an independent data-protection authority, are the main avenue for redress.[29][27][28][23]

The government’s 2020–2030 Digital Transformation Strategy identifies issuing implementing decrees for Law 81/2018 and fully enforcing the 2017/2021 Access to Information Law (Law 28/2017) as key reform priorities, but many decrees remain pending. Discussions at the 2024 Lebanon Internet Governance Forum stressed that data protection and cybersecurity laws need updates to align with best practice, including institutionalising data-protection officers and clearer governance rules.[28][30]

### Cybersecurity and cyber-governance

ITI’s 2024 Global Cybersecurity Index places Lebanon in "Tier 4 – evolving countries" with a score between 20 and 55, noting that the strongest pillar is legal measures (about 12.08/20) while technical, organizational, capacity-building, and cooperation measures lag behind. The National Cyber Security Index and other benchmarks track that Lebanon has a national cybersecurity strategy and a high-level national commission established under the prime minister’s office, but still lacks strong central leadership and implementation capacity.[31][32]

Internet Society Pulse cites a Global Cybersecurity Index security-preparedness score of around 32/100 and an e-government readiness score of about 54.49, underlining that cybersecurity and secure e-government are still at an early stage. At the 2024 Lebanon IGF, stakeholders described frequent malware and spyware attacks and a need for more robust defences and awareness programmes.[30][25]

Freedom on the Net 2024 and APC/SMEX’s 2025 UPR submission document persistent use of criminal defamation, insult, and cybercrime laws to target online speech, including posts criticising political elites or security forces. Authorities have repeatedly ordered internet content restrictions or platform blocks in response to security incidents or political pressures, though Lebanon has not implemented the long nationwide shutdowns seen in some neighbours.[21][23]

## Women’s rights and gender equality

Lebanon’s legal framework mixes areas of near equality with severe discrimination governed by religious personal-status laws. The World Bank’s Women, Business and the Law 2024 index assigns Lebanon an overall score of 58.8/100, well below the global average. It scores 100/100 on "mobility" and "workplace" (few formal legal restrictions on women’s movement or labour-force participation), but only 50 on "pay", 60 on "marriage", 20 on "parenthood", 75 on "entrepreneurship", 40 on "assets", and 25 on "pension".[33]

These low scores reflect structural issues: personal-status codes tied to religious communities govern marriage, divorce, custody, and inheritance; there is no unified civil code for family law. Human Rights Watch and others document that Lebanese authorities have failed to fully protect women from domestic violence and have not eliminated discrimination in citizenship transmission, property rights, and social protection, while migrant domestic workers fall outside labour-law protections under the kafala sponsorship system.[34][13][35]

Lebanon has a National Strategy for Women (2022–2030) and a National Commission for Lebanese Women (NCLW), and it acceded to CEDAW in 1997, but the national strategy’s own evaluation highlights that discriminatory laws and weak implementation remain major obstacles to gender equality. Equal Measures 2030’s 2024 SDG Gender Index includes Lebanon in its MENA coverage, showing that no country worldwide is on track to achieve gender equality by 2030 and that many, including Lebanon, have stagnated or regressed on key indicators.[36][37][38][35][34]

## LGBTQ+ rights and legal status

Lebanon does not explicitly criminalise same‑sex relations, but Article 534 of the Penal Code penalises "sexual intercourse contrary to nature" with up to one year in prison, and this has been used to prosecute LGBTQ+ people. Between 2007 and 2018 several lower courts ruled that consensual same‑sex relations are not "unnatural" and refused to apply Article 534, culminating in an August 2023 appeals‑court decision that consensual sex between people of the same sex is not unlawful. These rulings have created partial jurisprudential protection but have not repealed the article.[39][40][41][42]

In parallel, there has been a backlash. In June 2022 the Interior Minister issued a directive banning all events "promoting homosexuality"; although a court suspended that directive, the minister reissued a similar ban in November 2022. In August 2023 and 2024, members of parliament and the caretaker culture minister introduced bills that would explicitly criminalise same‑sex relations between consenting adults and penalise the "promotion" of homosexuality with prison terms and fines. Human Dignity Trust and ILGA’s country profiles classify Lebanon as criminalising same‑sex sexual activity between men and de facto targeting trans people’s gender expression, with no recognition of same‑sex unions, adoption, or anti‑discrimination protections based on sexual orientation or gender identity.[41][43][42][39]

## Data privacy, surveillance, and access to information

While Law 81/2018 nominally regulates personal-data processing, Lebanon lacks an independent data-protection authority; oversight is fragmented between ministries and courts, and many standard safeguards (data-breach notification, DPIAs, strong consent rules) are weak or absent compared to frameworks like the GDPR. Freedom on the Net 2024 flags the combination of state control over key ISPs and the absence of robust data-protection and surveillance-oversight mechanisms as enabling potential unlawful interception and retention of user data.[27][26][29][23]

The Access to Information Law (Law 28/2017, amended in 2021) obliges public bodies to proactively publish budgets and decisions and gives citizens the right to request information, including via online channels; later amendments removed restrictive capacity requirements for requesters. Implementation has been partial, with limited awareness, missing implementing decrees, and inconsistent record-keeping practices. Civil-society groups report frequent delays and refusals when requesting information on issues such as telecom spending or security-service practices.[28][21][30]

## Environment, ecology, and climate

Yale’s 2024 Environmental Performance Index (EPI) ranks Lebanon 126th of 180 countries with an overall score of 39.9/100, below the world average. Sub-index scores show particular weaknesses in ecosystem vitality, biodiversity and habitat (rank 161, score 23.6), terrestrial biome protection (rank 167), and pesticide pollution risk (rank 159), while fisheries performance is unusually high (rank 4, score 96.2) and water-resources management indicators sit around the global median.[4][44]

Lebanon’s national greenhouse-gas inventory to the UNFCCC reports total emissions of 20,519 Gg CO2-equivalent in 2022, down 32% from 2019 due mainly to the economic crisis and reduced energy-sector emissions. External estimates put 2021 emissions around 24.4 MtCO2e (0.05% of global emissions), or roughly 3.6 tonnes per capita, with about two-thirds from energy and the rest from industry, agriculture, and waste. Emissions had grown steadily since 1990 before the post‑2019 contraction.[45][6]

The Red Cross Red Crescent Climate Centre’s 2024 country profile highlights that, under high-emissions scenarios, Lebanon could face 11–21 very hot days above 35°C per year by mid‑century, with rainfall projected to decline by 4–11% by 2100 and water availability potentially dropping 29% by 2080. Floods currently occur 1–2 times a year and are expected to intensify, while snow cover could fall 40–70%, shrinking a key water-storage buffer. Lebanon ranks 49th out of 191 on the 2022 INFORM Risk Index, reflecting high exposure and vulnerability to natural hazards and climate impacts, exacerbated by the presence of large numbers of displaced people.[3]

Local environmental reporting notes that in the 2024 EPI Lebanon ranked around 124th–126th globally and 10th among 17 Arab countries, scoring 40.1 on the index and performing particularly poorly on terrestrial habitat and species protection; air pollution and wastewater management indicators are somewhat better but still below best practice. Chronic solid-waste mismanagement, untreated wastewater, and urban air pollution remain pressing issues.[46][4]

## Social protection, poverty, and inequality

The World Bank’s "Lebanon Poverty and Equity Assessment 2024" shows that 73% of Lebanese citizens and nearly all non‑citizens (mainly Syrian refugees) live in multidimensional poverty that includes deficits in electricity, healthcare, and education. Monetary poverty has tripled over the past decade and now affects 44% of the population, with particularly high rates in north Lebanon and among refugees.[13]

Gini coefficients around 31–35 (depending on source and year) and the concentration of wealth in banking, real estate, and political networks indicate deep structural inequality; the Bertelsmann Stiftung’s 2024 Transformation Index notes significant regional disparities and widespread tax evasion. In response to the crisis, the government and international partners have rolled out targeted safety-net programmes such as the National Poverty Targeting Programme and the Emergency Social Safety Net, but these rely on means-testing and reach only the poorest 20%, leaving many vulnerable households uncovered.[47][1][13]

In 2024 the government released its first National Social Protection Strategy, aiming to move from fragmented, donor-driven schemes to a more universal system, and has introduced new elements such as a disability allowance and a contributory pension reform. Implementation, however, will depend on fiscal space and governance reforms.[13]
