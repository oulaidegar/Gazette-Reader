all legal and administrative instruments in Lebanon. Established in 1922 during the French Mandate, it serves as the ultimate
source of legal authority; under Lebanese law, most statutes, legislative decrees, and ministerial decisions only become
legally binding and enforceable against the public after they have been published in the Gazette. It is typically published
weekly and contains a comprehensive record of the state's activities, including new laws passed by Parliament, executive
decrees from the President or Council of Ministers, official appointments, government tenders, and announcements related
to the commercial register and trademarks.

How it Works
Legal Force: Publication in the Gazette creates a legal presumption that everyone is aware of the law (the principle of
"ignorance of the law is no excuse"). Most laws specify a waiting period—often 30 days—between publication and
coming into force, unless an emergency status is granted.

📜 The Three Branches of Power
1. The Legislature (Parliament)
The Chamber of Deputies: 128 members elected every 4 years.
The 50/50 Split: Seats are divided exactly in half: 64 for Christians and 64 for Muslims.
The Goal: They pass laws, approve the national budget, and—most importantly—elect the President.

2. The Executive (The Cabinet)
Council of Ministers: Led by the Prime Minister.
Sectarian Balance: Like Parliament, the Cabinet must be "equitably" distributed among the sects.

Independent Courts: Based largely on the French civil law system.
Constitutional Council: Oversees the constitutionality of laws and settles election disputes.
Personal Status Courts: Uniquely, matters like marriage, divorce, and inheritance are handled by religious courts

The Taif Agreement (1989) ended the Lebanese Civil War and set the modern rules:
1. Parity: It established the equal 50:50 seat distribution in Parliament.
2. Executive Shift: It reduced the President's powers, making him more of a "referee" and the Prime Minister/Cabinet the

1. Centralization of State Authority

No. 2870 (December 16, 1959), Chehab wanted to move the "voice of the state" to the very center of executive power. By
placing it under the General Secretariat of the Cabinet, the government ensured that the publication of laws was directly
overseen by the highest executive body, rather than being siloed in a specific ministry.

2. The "Chehabist" Administrative Reform
President Chehab was obsessed with replacing Lebanon’s "clientelism" (politics based on favors) with a modern,
meritocratic "state of institutions."
Efficiency: He viewed the Ministry of Justice as being bogged down by judicial procedures. To modernize the country,
he needed a faster, more efficient way to disseminate administrative decisions.
The "Big Picture" Agencies: In 1959, Chehab created several "central control agencies" (like the Civil Service Board
and Central Inspection) that all reported to the Presidency of the Council of Ministers. Moving the Gazette there was
part of this "hub-and-spoke" model, where the Prime Minister’s office became the operational heart of the bureaucracy.

3. Legal Distinction: Law vs. Justice
The transfer clarified a conceptual difference in the Lebanese system:
The Ministry of Justice is responsible for the application of laws and the management of courts.
The Council of Ministers is responsible for the promulgation and execution of government policy.

Socio-Economic Trajectory and the Mechanics of Collapse
The Lebanese economic model, once centered on a robust banking sector and a vibrant service industry, effectively
disintegrated in late 2019. By 2024, the International Monetary Fund (IMF) and the World Bank identified Lebanon as
experiencing one of the deepest economic depressions in modern history, with GDP hovering around $28.3 billion—a
nominal figure that obscures a 40% real-term contraction from pre-crisis peaks.[1, 2]

Macroeconomic Instability and Financial Sector Losses
The financial sector's cumulative losses, exceeding $72 billion, have created a credit-starved environment where the
private sector is forced to operate on a cash-only basis.[1] While a temporary ceasefire in late 2024 led to a marginal uptick
in economic activity, the underlying infrastructure remains severely compromised. The World Bank’s Rapid Needs and
Damage Assessment in March 2025 estimated that recovery and reconstruction needs alone would require $11 billion in
immediate capital—a sum that is currently unavailable through domestic or traditional international channels due to stalled

The Legal Mosaic: Religious Autonomy vs. Individual Rights
Lebanon's legal framework is defined by Article 9 of the Constitution, which guarantees respect for the "personal status
and religious interests" of individuals.[13] This has resulted in a system where there is no unified civil code; instead, 15
separate religious laws govern family matters, including marriage, divorce, and child custody.[13, 14, 15]

Women’s Rights and the Sectarian Divide
Women in Lebanon are subject to a bifurcated legal reality where their rights depend entirely on their religious sect. Across
all denominations, personal status laws generally treat women as inferior to men, particularly regarding the right to divorce
and the guardianship of children.[13, 16, 17]

Marriage and Divorce Mechanics
In Muslim sects (Sunni, Shia, Druze), men possess an absolute, unilateral right to divorce (talaq) without needing to show
cause.[13, 17] Women, however, must prove specific grounds—such as abandonment or lack of financial support—and
often face lengthy, expensive litigation. While Sunni and Shia laws allow for a woman to hold the right to divorce ('isma) if it
is stipulated in the marriage contract, social taboos make this practice exceedingly rare.[17]
A critical legal vulnerability for women is the concept of "recalcitrance." If a woman leaves the marital home without a
reason deemed legitimate by a religious judge, she is declared "recalcitrant" and loses her right to spousal maintenance.[17,
18] This creates a legal trap for victims of domestic abuse, as religious courts do not always recognize physical violence as
a "legitimate" reason to leave the home if it does not meet a high threshold of proof.[16, 17]

Lebanese Official Gazette History Research

The Lebanese Official Gazette: A Socio-Legal and Administrative
History of Al-Jarida al-Rasmiya

The Lebanese Official Gazette, known in Arabic as Al-Jarida al-Rasmiya, constitutes the definitive legal record of the
Lebanese state, serving as the primary instrument for the promulgation of laws, decrees, and official notifications. Its
history is not merely a chronicle of administrative publishing but a mirror reflecting the broader political, legal, and social
transformations of Lebanon from the late Ottoman period through the French Mandate to the modern Republic. As the final,
indispensable stage in the legislative process, the Gazette provides the legal "notice to the world" that transforms a
proposed text into a binding reality for all citizens, embodying the principle that ignorance of the law is no excuse. This
report examines the evolution of the Gazette, its institutional management, and the contemporary challenges it faces within
a landscape marked by economic crisis and a growing demand for transparency.

Historical Foundations and Pre-Modern Precursors
The origins of official documentation in the territory of modern Lebanon are rooted in the late Ottoman Empire's
administrative reforms, known as the Tanzimat. During the 19th century, the Ottoman administration recognized the
necessity of a centralized press to communicate reforms and maintain provincial control over its diverse territories. In 1858,
Khalil al-Khuri launched Hadiqat al-Akhbar (The News Garden) in Beirut, which distinguished itself as the first private daily
newspaper in the city.[1, 2] While initially a private commercial venture, its significance was such that in 1860, at the request
of Fuad Pasha, the Ottoman Foreign Minister, it was elevated to a semi-official status following the communal conflicts in
Mount Lebanon and Damascus.[1]
This semi-official status allowed Hadiqat al-Akhbar to function as the primary vehicle for state announcements until the
launch of the strictly official Ottoman provincial publication Suriya.[1] Between 1869 and 1870, under the governorship of
Franco Pasha in the Mount Lebanon Mutasarrifate, the paper once again served as the semi-official voice of the
administration.[1] Simultaneously, the Mount Lebanon Mutasarrifate, established in 1861 as a semi-autonomous entity,
began producing its own specific documentation under the title Lubnan.[3, 4, 5] These early publications established the
precedent for a formal, centralized record of government activity that would eventually become a hallmark of the modern
Lebanese state.

The transition from the Ottoman administrative model to the French Mandate system following World War I brought
fundamental changes to the structure of official publishing. The State of Greater Lebanon was proclaimed in 1920, and the
evolving legal infrastructure necessitated a more rigorous bureaucratic framework. Decree No. 336 of September 1, 1920,
provisionally regulated the administrative organization of the state, identifying the "Department of Justice, Property and
Endowments" as one of the state's highest-ranking services.[6, 7] It was within this period of state-building that the Al-

Jarida al-Rasmiya was formally established in 1922, modeled largely after the French Journal Officiel to ensure systematic
publication of mandatory legal texts.[8, 9]

The Mandate Era and Constitutional Institutionalization
During the French Mandate (1920–1943), the Official Gazette became the definitive repository of the High Commissioner's
decisions and the legislative acts of the nascent Lebanese government. The legal weight of the Gazette was formally
codified in the Lebanese Constitution of May 23, 1926.[10, 11] Article 51 of the Constitution grants the President of the
Republic the power to promulgate laws after their passage by Parliament, but this promulgation is only finalized upon
publication in the Official Gazette.[11, 12]
The Mandate period was characterized by a rapid influx of legislative texts intended to modernize Lebanese society
according to French administrative norms. A prominent example is the legislation regulating Lebanese citizenship, issued as
a decision of the French High Commissioner in 1925, which remains the core of Lebanese nationality law today.[13]
Similarly, the Lebanese Commercial Code of 1942 (Decree No. 304) was published in the Gazette on April 7, 1943, marking
its formal entry into the legal corpus.[14] These examples highlight the Gazette's role as the "legal anchor" for the state,
providing a preserved record that allows the public to access the foundational rules of their society.

The 1959 Administrative Shift and the Presidency of the Council of Ministers
A pivotal moment in the Gazette’s institutional history occurred in 1959. Prior to this, the management, printing, and
distribution of the Gazette were handled by the "Committee of Litigation and Legislation" within the Ministry of Justice.[6,
15] However, as part of a broader administrative reorganization under President Fouad Chehab, Decree No. 2870 of
December 16, 1959, transferred the management of the Official Gazette to the Presidency of the Council of Ministers (PCM).
[6]
This transfer moved the responsibility to the General Secretariat of the Presidency of the Cabinet, which assumed the tasks
of publication, printing, distribution, and archiving of all texts to be integrated.[6] This shift reflected the increasing
importance of the Prime Minister’s office in the post-independence era, particularly as the coordinator of ministerial
activities and the overseer of the "good performance" of public administrations.[16, 17] The Gazette thereby became an
essential tool for the Prime Minister to ensure that all governmental branches operated under a unified and publicly
accessible set of promulgated rules.

The Gazette’s Legal Purpose and Social Context
The fundamental purpose of the Lebanese Official Gazette is to satisfy the democratic principle of "legality." For any law,
decree, or administrative decision to be enforceable, the public must have a reasonable means of accessing it. This is a
constitutional mandate: the Constitution stipulates that certain "basic national issues," such as changes to personal status
laws, the national budget, or international treaties, require a high degree of transparency and must be documented and
published in the Gazette.[11, 12]
The content of the Gazette is exhaustive and includes:
Legislative laws passed by the Chamber of Deputies.
Regulatory and organizational decrees issued by the President and Prime Minister.
Decisions from the Council of Ministers.
Official declarations from public administrations, institutions, and municipalities.
Commercial register announcements and lawsuit notices.[8, 10]

In the Lebanese social context, the Gazette is a critical resource for legal professionals. Without it, the "judicial precedents"
and "fundamental perspectives" required for legal practice would be impossible to track.[18] Furthermore, the Gazette is
the only venue where international treaties ratified by Lebanon are made publicly available, a necessary step for these
agreements to be integrated into the domestic legal system and to be invoked in Lebanese courts.[11, 12]

Challenges During the Lebanese Civil War (1975–1990)
The fifteen-year Lebanese Civil War (1975–1990) was an unprecedented conflict that fractured the state administrative
bureaucracy and divided the country into cantons controlled by various militias.[19, 20] Despite this fragmentation, the
Lebanese state maintained a veneer of continuity through its official institutions, including the Official Gazette. While the
stability of the state was deeply compromised, the publication of the Gazette continued, though its actual enforcement was
often limited to the territories where the central government maintained some degree of control.[20]
The post-war recovery period, initiated by the Taif Agreement in 1989, utilized the existing administrative framework of the
Gazette to rebuild state legitimacy. The Taif Agreement itself, and the subsequent constitutional amendments of 1990, were
published in the Gazette to signal the return to constitutional order.[21, 22] This era underscored the importance of the
Gazette as a permanent legal record that can bridge periods of societal collapse, providing the documentary basis for
reconstruction and the restoration of state institutions.

The Digital Transformation and the IDS Portal Breakthrough
The arrival of the 21st century brought new opportunities for the Al-Jarida al-Rasmiya through digitalization. The transition
from physical paper to digital archives was seen as a way to solve the historical problems of limited public access and the
physical degradation of paper records. Until 2005, the Gazette remained available primarily in its printed version.[23] In
2005, the website of the Presidency of the Council of Ministers began publishing digital versions, making current and past
issues accessible to the public for the first time without requiring a visit to a physical government office.[23]
A major technological breakthrough occurred in May 2016 when Integrated Digital Systems (IDS) launched a central online
database portal designed to digitize the laws of the Official Gazette dating back to 1915.[24] This portal, located at
legallaw.ul.edu.lb, was intended to serve as a central repository for both legislations and court rulings, featuring a
modernized search interface specifically for legal professionals and researchers.[24, 25]

Problems and Complaints: The Paywall Controversy
The most significant setback to the public's "right to know the law" occurred in 2018. Prior to this, the online version of the
Gazette was available free of charge on the PCM website.[23, 27] However, in a move to generate revenue for the state
treasury, the authorities introduced a paywall for electronic access, setting a yearly subscription fee of LL 550,000.[23, 27]
This decision was met with widespread public and professional complaints. Critics identified several "illogical" aspects of
the paywall:
1. Inverse Pricing Structure: The yearly membership for the printed version of the Gazette was set at LL 240,000, while
the online version cost more than double that amount at LL 550,000.[27] This discouraged the transition to digital
formats and penalized those seeking more efficient access to information.
2. Fundamental Right of Access: Legal scholars and civil society groups argued that legislative texts should be public
information accessible to all for free, particularly since the state requires citizens to comply with these very laws.[27]

3. Revenue vs. Public Service: The move was widely seen as an attempt to "feed the treasury" during a financial crisis
rather than a genuine effort to improve public service or administrative efficiency.[27]
4. Technical Inadequacies: Despite the high fee, the "official" digital versions provided by the government were often
paradoxically not searchable, downloadable, or copyable in their free preview formats, making them functionally
useless for serious legal research without the paid subscription.[23]
Even high-ranking officials, including the Prime Minister at the time, reportedly characterized the new fee as "expensive,"
yet the paywall remained in place.[27] This created a "gatekeeping" effect, where real-time access to changes in Lebanese
law became a privilege reserved for those who could afford it, a particularly problematic development during a period of
rapid legislative change in response to the economic collapse.

The Right to Access Information and Civil Society Activism
The 2018 paywall stood in direct conflict with the landmark Law No. 28 of 2017, known as the "Right to Access Information
Law." This law was designed to enhance transparency by obliging state administrations to publish their budgets, decisions,
and administrative memos online in searchable and copyable formats.[28, 29, 30] Organizations such as the Gherbal
Initiative and the Legal Agenda have been at the forefront of the battle to ensure the implementation of this law.
In 2021, the Access to Information Law was amended via Law 233 to explicitly state that individuals requesting information
do not need to justify their interest or reveal their identity.[28] Crucially, the amendment required that all documents
previously mandated for publication in the Official Gazette must also be made available on the respective administrations'
websites for free in an open-access, searchable, and downloadable format.[23, 28]
Despite these legal victories, implementation has been inconsistent. A 2018 study by the Gherbal Initiative found a
significant lack of compliance: of 133 public bodies sent information requests, only 18 provided the requested data.[31]
Furthermore, high-level officials have occasionally refused information requests by arguing that the law is
"unimplementable" without further applicatory decrees, a claim that legal watchdogs and the Ministry of Justice's own
committees have rejected as a "neutralization" of the law.[32, 33]

The Role of Watchdog Organizations in Data Preservation
Given the barriers to accessing the Official Gazette through formal government channels, civil society has stepped in to
create "shadow archives." The Gherbal Initiative, for example, has played a vital role in monitoring the implementation of
public procurement laws through data painstakingly collected from the Gazette and other sources.[34] The Legal Agenda
has similarly utilized the Gazette as a primary source for its "LAPOLeb" platform, which monitors parliamentary activity and
ensure that published laws are consistent with citizens' fundamental rights.[35]
These organizations provide a crucial service by making legal data more accessible and searchable than the official PCM
portal currently allows. Their work highlights the transition of the Official Gazette from a passive administrative record to an
active site of struggle over transparency, accountability, and the rule of law in Lebanon.

The Impact of the Economic Crisis on Physical Publication
The ongoing economic crisis has further complicated the management of the Official Gazette. The collapse of the
Lebanese pound and the resulting hyperinflation have rendered the physical printing of the Gazette increasingly difficult.
Reports from 2022 indicated that the government faced severe paper and ink shortages, occasionally threatening to halt
the physical printing of the Gazette altogether.[10, 36]
This "paper crisis" has pushed the necessity for a reliable, free digital version to the forefront of administrative reform
discussions. However, the government's continued reliance on the paywall as a source of revenue, combined with the lack
of staff in key oversight bodies like the Court of Audit, has created a situation where legal publication is often delayed or
technically flawed.[37] The economic situation has thus become both a driver for digital reform and a primary obstacle to
its successful implementation.

Institutional Memory and the Judicial Gazette
While the Al-Jarida al-Rasmiya focuses on administrative and legislative texts, the "Judicial Gazette," managed by the
Ministry of Justice, provides a specialized layer of official documentation. Established in 1944, it focuses on jurisprudence

and court decisions.[18] The history of the Judicial Gazette mirrors the Official Gazette in its struggles: it ceased regular
publication in 1998 due to "important problems" within the Ministry, including staff shortages and a lack of funding.[18]
Efforts to relaunch the Judicial Gazette in 2003 as an electronic copy faced similar bureaucratic hurdles as the Official
Gazette's digital transition.[18] The parallel decline of both gazettes suggests a broader systemic erosion of Lebanon’s
"institutional memory." When the state fails to publish its laws and the courts fail to publish their precedents in a timely and
accessible manner, the rule of law is weakened, and power is exercised with decreasing transparency.

Conclusions and Future Outlook
The Lebanese Official Gazette, Al-Jarida al-Rasmiya, remains the indispensable pillar of the Lebanese legal system, yet it is
currently a site of profound institutional stress. From its roots in the Ottoman Tanzimat and its formalization during the
French Mandate, the Gazette has evolved from a semi-official newspaper to a constitutional requirement for the validity of
state acts. Its transfer to the Presidency of the Council of Ministers in 1959 signaled a centralization of executive authority
that continues to define its management today.
However, the introduction of the 2018 paywall and the inconsistent implementation of the 2017 Access to Information Law
have created a crisis of access. For the Gazette to fulfill its democratic purpose, several key reforms are necessary:
The removal of digital paywalls: Recognizing that access to the law is a fundamental right that should not be
commodified.
Technological modernization: Upgrading the official portals to ensure that all gazetted texts are searchable, copyable,
and downloadable, as mandated by Law 233/2021.
Archival continuity: Resolving the staff and funding shortages that prevent the timely publication of both the Official
and Judicial Gazettes.
The story of the Gazette is ultimately the story of Lebanon's ongoing struggle for a transparent and accountable state. As
civil society continues to pressure the government for greater openness, the Official Gazette will remain a primary indicator
of the state's commitment to the rule of law and the fundamental rights of its citizens. The transition from a "militia republic"
to a modern, transparent democracy requires that the law be not just written, but made universally accessible to all who are
subject to its authority.
